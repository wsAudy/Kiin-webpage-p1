The Gap

Two people get the same diagnosis on the same day.

One is open by nature. Voices what she feels, what she needs, what she's afraid of, without hesitation. She's almost never alone. Surrounded by people ready to listen, who check in on her daily, loved ones who take leave to accompany her to every appointment. She happens to have a friend who's been through it before. A doctor who shared her WhatsApp number, reachable any time she's afraid.

The other is private by nature. Carries her fear quietly, the way she's carried everything: on her own, without asking, without needing to “trouble” anyone else. She is mostly alone, holds herself to unreal thresholds, enduring the way she's endured most other things, not knowing where to draw the line. Her doctor is doing their best in fifteen-minute windows, working only with what they're told.

Both are doing everything ‘right’, the best way they naturally would.

One happens to have the ‘right’ personality, the ‘right’ actively engaged support, the ‘right’ doctor.

The other is doing her best too, yet unknowingly slipping through a gap she and her loved ones didn't see coming.

Right now, which path you get to be on isn't something you chose. Your personality. Who happens to be around you. Which doctor you happen to get.

None of it was chosen. Therein the gap.

Behind Kiin

It took thirty years to appreciate what science alone cannot fix.

I started as a biology and engineering student who became a scientist, convinced that science was the path to solving almost any problem, including curing disease. Decades later, after working across research, biomedical devices, commercial and clinical systems, I saw how long the road is from discovery to solutions that actually work for the people who need them.

The thing I could not solve with logic was not in a lab or a boardroom. It was at home.

My mother had cancer.

I read every one of her lab reports. I watched her put on a brave front, then collapse behind it. I saw some of it coming and had no way to stop it. Worse were the things I did not see coming: assumptions that worked against us without anyone noticing, incomplete truths that gave us false reassurance, a full picture that no one held because all of us: the family and the system, were exhausting everything we had, grasping at the wrong straws.

A year passed after my mom’s successful surgery with monthly check‑ups that flagged nothing. She collapsed one day and at that point, tumors were found in her brain, lungs, bones, and liver.

She passed. What hit me was not only the loss, but the profound sense of failure I carried. As a daughter, as a scientist, and part of a family navigating a system we didn't fully understand. The miscommunications. The things we did not know how to say to each other. The weight of being caregivers without any real preparation for what that truly meant, until it was too late.

Everyone’s experience is unique. But the pattern is not. Patients carrying things they never found a reason or a way to say. Families holding knowledge they did not know was worth sharing. Systems trying to treat diseases while whole persons fall through the cracks.

I cannot stop asking one question: what would it take for patients and families to truly be seen, as whole people, by those who care for them and by the systems trying to treat and support them?

Kiin is my commitment to formally pursue that question. I do not hold full answers, but am starting with a conviction to understand and to help bridge the gap I experienced first‑hand, and a desire to listen and learn from others.

If you have been through this, your experience will help shape what Kiin becomes.

— Founder, Kiin
